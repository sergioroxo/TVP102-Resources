Shot Sizes
Standard framing — use these terms on your shot list and on set
EWS Extreme Wide Shot Subject tiny in landscape. Establishes place, scale, isolation.
WS Wide / Full Shot Full body with space around. Shows subject in environment.
MLS Medium Long Shot Knees to top of head. Character and some environment.
MS Medium Shot Waist to top of head. Most common dialogue shot.
MCU Medium Close-Up Chest to top of head. Conversational intimacy.
CU Close-Up Face fills frame. Emotion and reaction.
ECU Extreme Close-Up Single feature: eyes, hands, mouth. Intensity and detail.
OTS Over-the-Shoulder Camera behind one character looking at another.
POV Point of View Camera = character's exact eyeline. Subjective.
INS Insert Shot Close-up of object carrying story information.
2S Two-Shot Two subjects in frame. Relationship and dynamic.
EST Establishing Shot Opens a scene. Shows where and when we are.

The 180° Rule: Draw an imaginary line through your two subjects. Keep the camera on one side of this line for the entire scene. Crossing it disorients the viewer spatially — this is a continuity error, not a style choice.

Camera Movement
Note movement type on every shot in your shot list
STATIC
Camera locked off on tripod. No movement at all. The dominant shot type in professional cinema — stillness is power, not laziness.
↳ Default choice. Always ask: does this shot need to move?
PAN
Camera head rotates horizontally left or right on a fixed tripod. Follows action, reveals space, connects subjects. Camera does not translate — it pivots.
↳ Pan speed matters enormously. Too fast = whip pan. Too slow = uncomfortable.
TILT
Camera head rotates vertically up or down on a fixed tripod. Reveals scale (tilt up a building), follows action vertically, creates dread or awe.
↳ Often combined with other movements — tilt + pan simultaneously.
DOLLY
Camera physically moves through space on a wheeled cart along tracks. Moving the entire camera toward (push in) or away (pull out) from subject. Creates parallax — background shifts relative to foreground.
↳ Zoom changes focal length. Dolly changes perspective. Not the same thing.
TRACK
Camera moves laterally alongside a moving subject — parallel tracking. Keeps subject in frame while background flows past. Feeling of accompaniment, momentum.
↳ Tracking shots require laid track or a very smooth floor. Plan ahead.
HANDHELD
Operator holds camera without stabilisation. Introduces organic wobble, energy, urgency, or unease. The camera becomes a participant in the scene, not an observer.
↳ Intentional handheld ≠ accidentally shaky. Decide before you pick it up.
STEADICAM
Camera mounted on a spring-loaded vest rig worn by the operator. Smooth, gliding movement that follows action through complex spaces. Neither locked-off static nor unstable handheld — its own distinct vocabulary.
↳ Requires specialist operator. Don't improvise this on shoot day.
CRANE / JIB
Camera elevated on a counterbalanced arm. Reveals environment from above, suggests scale, provides God's-eye view. Often used at the end of a scene to pull away from characters.
↳ Even a simple jib arm creates dramatic elevation changes in small spaces.
WHIP PAN
An extremely fast pan that blurs the frame during movement. Used as a transition between scenes or to suggest rapid disorientation. Edgar Wright uses it extensively for comedic and rhythmic effect.
↳ Match direction of blur when cutting to a reverse whip pan in editing.
DUTCH ANGLE
Camera tilted on its longitudinal axis — the horizon is diagonal in frame. Suggests psychological unease, instability, moral ambiguity. A stylistic choice, not a correctable error.
↳ Use sparingly — it draws attention to itself. Earn it with context.
On-Set Roles
Who does what — and who answers to whom

Chain of command: On set, every crew member takes direction from their department head. Don't give notes across departments — that's the Director and 1st AD's job. If something is wrong, tell your department head first.

DIRECTION
Director Overall creative authority. Communicates vision to all departments. Gives direction to actors. The only person who says "Cut" (or delegates this to 1st AD).
1st AD Runs the floor day-to-day. Calls "Action", "Cut", "Rolling". Manages schedule, safety, and communication between departments. If the Director is the creative brain, the 1st AD is the operational spine.
PRODUCTION
Producer Manages logistics, budget, and schedule off-camera. Problem-solves before problems reach the set. Produces the call sheet with the 1st AD.
Script Supervisor Monitors continuity shot-to-shot. Logs completed takes, circle takes, and timing. The editor's best friend — their notes are what the edit room works from.
CAMERA
DP / DoP Director of Photography. Responsible for all visual and lighting decisions. Translates the director's vision into specific shots, lighting setups, and lens choices.
Camera Operator Physically operates the camera during takes under the DP's guidance. On small productions, the DP often also operates.
1st AC (Focus Puller) Manages focus during shots, measures actor distances, and maintains the camera. One of the most technically demanding on-set roles.
DIT / Data Wrangler Manages all media cards and file backup during shooting. Never leave a shoot day without two copies of the footage on separate drives.
LIGHTING & GRIP
Gaffer Chief electrician. Executes the DP's lighting plan. Responsible for all electrical safety on set.
Key Grip Manages all non-electrical physical support: dolly tracks, rigging, camera mounts, reflectors, flags. Works closely with the Gaffer.
SOUND
Sound Recordist / Mixer Captures all on-set audio. Monitors dialogue and ambient levels. Sets recording levels and manages the audio recording device.
Boom Operator Holds and positions the boom microphone to capture dialogue as close as possible without entering frame. A skilled boom op is invisible but invaluable.
ART DEPARTMENT
Production Designer Responsible for the entire visual world of the film: sets, props, dressing. Works from the director's vision and the DP's lighting plan.
Costume Designer All wardrobe decisions. Maintains costume continuity between scenes — especially critical when scenes are shot out of order.
Set Commands
The language of the floor — know these before day one
Shooting Sequence
QUIET ON SET Called by 1st AD before a take. All talking, phones, and movement stops immediately.
TURNOVER / ROLL 1st AD tells camera and sound to begin recording. Both must confirm before action is called.
ROLLING Camera operator confirms the camera is recording. "Rolling" = camera is running.
SPEED Sound Recordist confirms audio is recording. Comes after "Rolling", before "Action".
MARK IT Clapperboard is hit in front of camera to create a sync point for picture and sound in post.
ACTION Director (or 1st AD at director's instruction) calls this. Actors begin the scene. Not before.
CUT Director ends the take. No one moves until this is called. Camera and sound keep running until "Cut".
CHECK THE GATE After the last take of a setup, the 1st AC inspects the camera for dust or debris. Nothing moves until this is cleared.
MOVING ON 1st AD confirms the gate is good and the shot is complete. Crew begins striking and setting up the next shot.
BACK TO ONE Reset. Actors return to their starting positions. Crew resets props and continuity to the top of the scene.

Shutter Speed = Frame Rate × 2

Depth of Field Factors
APERTURE Wider aperture (lower f-stop) = shallower DOF. Most direct control over background blur.
FOCAL LENGTH Longer lens (85mm, 135mm) = shallower DOF. Wide lens (24mm) = deeper DOF at same f-stop.
DISTANCE Closer subject to camera = shallower DOF. Move in before reaching for a wider aperture.
SENSOR SIZE Full-frame sensors produce shallower DOF than crop sensors at equivalent settings.
Lighting
Types, positions, and practical setups
Three-Point Lighting

The foundation of narrative lighting. Not a formula to follow blindly — a starting point to understand and then break deliberately. Key → Fill → Back/Rim. Master this and you can light anything.

KEY LIGHT
Primary light source. Determines the direction and mood of the scene. Usually 45° to one side and slightly above the subject. The dominant light.
FILL LIGHT
Softens the shadow created by the key. Placed on the opposite side, lower intensity. Controls contrast ratio — how dark the shadow side is.
BACK / RIM LIGHT
Behind and above the subject, aimed at their shoulders and hair. Separates them from the background. Creates depth. Often the most cinematic of the three.
PRACTICAL
A light source visible in frame — a lamp, a window, a screen. Also used to mean any on-set light motivated by a visible source in the scene world.
BOUNCE
Light reflected off a white card, foam board, or reflector. Softer, more diffuse than direct light. Free and available — a bounce board changes everything.
NEGATIVE FILL
A black card placed to subtract light from the shadow side of a face, increasing contrast. Darkens without adding a light source.
Light Quality
HARD LIGHT
Small, direct source creates sharp-edged shadows. Dramatic, high-contrast. Direct sun, bare bulb, undiffused LED.
↳ Use for tension, intensity, noir. Unflattering on skin at close range.
SOFT LIGHT
Large or diffused source wraps around subject, creates gradual shadows. Flattering, gentle. Overcast sky, light through diffusion, bounced light.
↳ Larger the source relative to subject = softer the light.
Contrast Ratio

The ratio between key and fill light. 1:1 = flat, even (news broadcast). 2:1 = gentle modelling. 4:1 = dramatic narrative look. 8:1+ = noir / high contrast. Know your ratio before you light.

Continuity Checklist
Check these between every take — tap to mark as done, reset between setups
Costume & Props
Clothing position — collar, buttons, jacket lapels, sleeves. Are they exactly as they were at the start of the scene?
Hair — loose strands, parting, position of any accessories. Check against last shot's reference photo.
Props in hand — which hand, how held, level of any drink in a glass, state of any food item.
Props on set — position of every object in frame. Take a reference photo before every setup change.
Jewellery & accessories — rings, watches, glasses, earrings. These disappear between takes more often than anything else.
Performance
Action matches — if cutting mid-action (picking up a cup, turning to look), the action must match from both angles precisely.
Eyeline — actors must look at the same point in space across angles. Mark an eyeline target if needed.
180° rule respected — all camera positions on the same side of the action line throughout the scene.
Technical
Exposure locked — if shooting near a window, block light changes or use manual exposure to prevent flicker between takes.
White balance set manually — auto WB drifts. Set a fixed value at the start of each lighting setup.
Sound speed confirmed — "Speed" called before every "Action". Never start a take without audio rolling.
Clapper board updated — scene number, shot number, and take number correct before every take.
Media card capacity — check remaining recording time before a long take. Back up completed cards before they're reused.
End of Setup
Check the gate — 1st AC inspects camera before moving on. Never strike a setup before this is done.
Reference photo taken — script supervisor or continuity photographs actors and set before any changes.
Script supervisor notes updated — take numbers, circle takes, timing, and any continuity notes logged before moving on.
Flicker & White Balance
The two most common technical disasters in student films — both 100% preventable
Why Flicker Happens

The problem: Artificial lights (LED, fluorescent, tungsten) cycle on and off with the electrical mains — 50 times per second in Norway and Europe (50Hz). If your shutter speed doesn't divide evenly into 50, the camera catches the light at different points in its cycle from frame to frame. In the edit, this looks like a slow or fast pulsing flicker that is impossible to fix in post.

The Fix — Norway/Europe (50Hz Mains)
SAFE ✓
Use shutter speeds that divide cleanly into 50: 1/50, 1/100, 1/200, 1/500, 1/1000
↳ 1/50 is your everyday setting at 25fps. 1/100 for slow-motion at 50fps.
DANGER ✗
Avoid: 1/48, 1/60, 1/125, 1/250 — these do not divide into 50 and will cause flicker under artificial lights.
↳ 1/48 is common in US camera defaults (for 24fps NTSC). Check your camera menu.

General Rule
Norway / Europe = 50Hz mains Always shoot at 25fps + 1/50 shutter indoors.

If you need slow motion: shoot 50fps + 1/100 shutter, play back at 25fps = 2× slow motion.

USA/Canada = 60Hz. If collaborating with US footage, their safe shutters are 1/60, 1/120. Different country = different rule.
LED Flickering (High-Speed & PWM)

Modern LED lights (including cheap panel LEDs on location) often use Pulse Width Modulation (PWM) to dim — they flicker at very high frequencies invisible to the eye but visible at higher shutter speeds. If you see banding in your footage even with correct shutter: dim your LEDs less, or raise your LED's frequency if it has that setting. Cheap LEDs at 50% brightness flicker more than the same LED at full power.

White Balance — The Rules

Never use Auto White Balance (AWB) during a shoot. AWB recalculates continuously — it will drift between takes, making shots cut together look like different times of day. One take will look warm, the next cool, and it's extremely difficult to match in the colour grade.

STEP 1
Identify your dominant light source — is the scene primarily daylight (window), tungsten (warm household bulbs), or LED/fluorescent? You're setting WB to match the main source.
STEP 2
Set a manual Kelvin value or use a preset. Daylight: 5500K. Overcast: 6500K. Tungsten: 3200K. LED varies — start at 5000K and adjust.
↳ C300: WB button → Custom → point at grey card → Set. R6: Q menu → WB → Custom.
STEP 3
Lock it and don't touch it for the duration of that lighting setup. When you move to a different location or the light changes significantly, repeat steps 1–2.
↳ On iPhone: tap and hold to lock AE/AF, then adjust exposure wheel. WB is auto but locking exposure helps consistency.
PRO TIP
Shoot a grey card at the start of each setup. Hold a grey card in front of the camera for 3 seconds before the first take. In DaVinci Resolve you can use this to auto-correct WB in post with one click.
↳ No grey card? A white piece of paper works at a pinch. Even a grey hoodie. The goal is a known neutral reference.
Mixed Lighting — What to Do

The hardest situation: a room with a window (daylight, ~5500K) and ceiling lights (tungsten or LED, ~3200K). You can't set WB to match both. Solutions in order of preference: (1) turn off the ceiling lights and use only window light, (2) gel the window with CTO to warm it to match indoor lights, (3) gel indoor lights with CTB to cool them to match daylight, (4) set WB to match the dominant source and accept a colour cast on the other — you can partially correct in grade. Never leave it on AWB.

Audio Levels
Get this wrong and no amount of post-production will save you

The cardinal rule: Bad audio cannot be fixed. Bad picture can sometimes be saved. Always monitor audio through headphones during recording — never guess.

The Level Meter — What You're Reading
-40 -18 TARGET -12 -6 0 CLIP
■ Safe: below -12 dBFS ■ Caution: -12 to -6 dBFS ■ Danger: above -6 / Clip: 0 dBFS
-18 dBFS
Target level for dialogue. Average speech should peak around here. This gives you 18dB of headroom before clipping. European broadcast standard for dialogue.
↳ Also written as -18 dBu or 0 VU in older equipment. They mean the same reference point.
-12 dBFS
Maximum average for loud moments — shouting, emotional peaks. Occasional transients can go higher briefly, but sustained levels here mean your dialogue is too loud.
-6 dBFS
Warning zone. Peaks here occasionally are acceptable, but if your meter is consistently hitting -6 you are too loud and will clip on loud transients.
0 dBFS
Clipping. Distortion. Cannot be fixed. Digital audio that hits 0 dBFS creates square-wave clipping — a harsh crackling distortion that is permanent. Set your levels before recording. Never let peaks reach 0.
↳ If in doubt, record 6dB quieter. Noise is fixable. Clipping is not.

Common Audio Disasters — Prevention
CLIPPING
Cause: Gain too high. Loud moment sends signal above 0 dBFS. Fix: Lower gain. Enable dual recording. Test levels before rolling.
NO SYNC
Cause: Forgot to clap the board. Sound and picture don't align. Fix: Always slate every take. If you forget: clap hands visibly in front of the camera at the start or end of the take as an emergency sync point.
HANDLING NOISE
Cause: Boom operator moves, cable rustles, hand rubs mic. Fix: Hold the boom steady. Use a proper shock mount. Dress cables so they don't rub clothing.
ROOM REVERB
Cause: Bare room with hard surfaces — bathrooms, kitchens, empty offices. Fix: Add soft furnishings, hang blankets off-camera, get the boom closer to the actor. You cannot remove reverb in post.
NO ROOM TONE
Cause: Forgetting to record room tone at the end of each location. Fix: At the end of every location, record 30–60 seconds of silence. This is the ambient sound of the room. Editors cannot live without it.

Sensor Sizes & Crop Factors
Why the same lens looks different on different cameras — and on your phone

The reference point: All crop factors are measured against Full Frame (35mm) — the size of a 35mm film frame (36 × 24 mm). A crop factor of 1.5× means a 50mm lens behaves like a 75mm lens would on a full-frame camera. You see less of the scene, but the perspective is identical.

Sensor Sizes — Department Cameras
Canon C300 Mark II & III
Super 35mm
Sensor: 26.2 × 13.8 mm
Crop factor: ≈ 1.46–1.53× (varies by recording resolution)
Super 35 is the cinema standard — the same as a 4-perf 35mm film frame. Your 24mm lens behaves like ~35mm full frame. Your 50mm behaves like ~73mm. Very close to APS-C but with a cinematic pedigree.
Canon EOS R, R6, R6 Mark II, EOS R5 IV
Full Frame
Sensor: 35.9 × 23.9 mm
Crop factor: 1.0× (no crop)
Full frame = the reference. A 50mm is a 50mm. Widest field of view, shallowest depth of field per f-stop, best low-light performance. When using EF-S lenses, the camera auto-crops to 1.6× APS-C mode — avoid this for narrative work as it dramatically reduces resolution.
Canon EOS 6D & 6D Mark II
Full Frame
Sensor: 35.8 × 23.9 mm
Crop factor: 1.0× (no crop)
Full frame DSLR — same field of view as the R-series. Uses EF lenses natively. Video is limited to 1080p (no 4K). Good for stills reference shots and B-camera roles. The 6D Mark II adds Dual Pixel AF which helps with video focus.
iPhone 14 / 15 / 16 (all models)
1/1.28" sensor
Sensor: ≈ 9.8 × 7.3 mm (main camera)
Crop factor: ≈ 3.5–4.0× vs full frame
Apple reports focal lengths in full-frame equivalent — so "24mm" on iPhone is already the equivalent view. The physical lens is ~6.8mm. Depth of field is very deep even wide open — near-impossible to get shallow focus. Compensate with close proximity to subject and strong background separation in framing.
Drones (DJI Mini 4 Pro / Air 3)
1/1.3" sensor
Sensor: ≈ 9.4 × 7.1 mm
Crop factor: ≈ 3.7× vs full frame
Drone sensors are close to iPhone in size. Small sensor = very deep depth of field = everything in focus = cinematic look requires different tools (movement, altitude change, framing). Always fly at 25fps/1/50 shutter in Norway. D-Log M colour profile gives more grading flexibility.

Depth of Field — Sensor Size Effect

Larger sensors produce shallower depth of field at the same equivalent field of view and aperture. This is why the C300 or R6 at f/2.8 blurs backgrounds more than an iPhone at "f/1.8" — the iPhone's physical aperture is very small relative to its sensor. For the cinematic background-blur look, use the C300 or R6 with fast lenses (f/1.4–f/2.8) and keep some distance between subject and background.

Documentary & Interview
Non-fiction filmmaking — observational, interview, essay film, vox pop
Documentary Modes
Observational
Camera watches without intervening. No narrator, no direct address. The unplanned moment is the material. Direct cinema tradition — Wiseman, Maysles.
↳ Small crew, follow the action, accept imperfection. Presence shapes what you find.
Participatory / Interview
Filmmaker asks questions, prompts subjects, sometimes appears on screen. The sit-down interview is the primary structural device.
↳ Interview + B-roll cutaways. Ask open questions — "Tell me about…" not yes/no.
Essay Film
First-person voice explores ideas through images and narration. Subjective, associative, question-driven. Varda, Marker, Steyerl.
↳ Images don't have to illustrate the narration — they can contradict or rhyme with it.
Vox Pop / Street Interview
Short on-the-street opinion interviews with the public. Fast to shoot. Requires editorial instinct — most material won't be usable.
↳ Shoot 6–10 to get 3–4 usable ones. Pick your location before approaching subjects.
Interview Framings
Standard camera setups for sit-down, street, and environmental interviews
OTS INT Interview OTS Camera behind interviewer. Subject looks slightly off-lens. Feels conversational. Standard documentary framing.
DIR CAM Direct to Camera Subject looks into lens. Confessional, essay film, vox pop. Rare in narrative fiction — creates immediacy and intimacy.
EYE RM Eye Room Subject left or right of frame with space on the side they're looking toward. Always leave eye room in interview framings.
2S INT Two-Shot Interview Both subjects in frame, looking toward each other. Couple or partner interviews. Keep both faces visible — avoid full profiles.
ENV INT Environmental MS or WS placing subject in their context — office, lab, studio. Background tells the story. Best for documentary subjects where place adds meaning.
WALK INT Walking Interview Handheld follows subject while moving. Creates energy, reveals environment. Use wireless lavalier. Good for street documentary and vox pop.

Eyeline rule: In a sit-down interview, the subject looks just past the lens — at the interviewer seated directly beside or just behind the camera. Result: not looking at camera (journalistic distance) but engaged with someone (not staring into space). The closer the interviewer is to the lens axis, the more natural the eyeline appears on screen.

Interview Setup — Step by Step
LOCATION
Choose a location that tells a story. Where does the subject live, work, or belong? Do a recce first and listen carefully — a humming fridge or open window can ruin audio.
↳ Avoid bare white walls unless intentional. Environment adds meaning.
FRAMING
MCU or MS. Subject left or right of frame with eye room. Eyes on the upper third. Background slightly soft if possible. Check carefully for anything "growing" out of the subject's head.
LIGHTING
Window light as key is excellent — supplement with a reflector on the shadow side. Avoid harsh overhead fluorescents. Turn off ceiling lights if possible and let window or LED do the work.
↳ Set a manual white balance to match your dominant source. Don't mix daylight and tungsten uncontrolled.
AUDIO
Run boom AND lavalier simultaneously. The boom gives natural room tone; the lavalier gives clean close-up when the subject turns their head. Choose in editing — often you'll blend both.
↳ Record one full minute of room tone before and after. You will need it.
B-ROLL
Shoot B-roll after the interview while the subject is still there. Listen to the transcript and note every noun — that's your B-roll list. You need 3–5× more than you think.
↳ The edit lives and dies on your B-roll. Prioritise activity shots over cutaways of the subject just sitting.
B-Roll — Types
ACTIVITY Subject doing what they're talking about. Most powerful B-roll — action and narration reinforce each other.
DETAIL Close-ups of hands, tools, textures. Slows pace, creates intimacy. Often the most cinematic B-roll you'll shoot.
ESTABLISH Wide shots of location, street, building. Grounds the viewer in space. Used at the start of a sequence or to transition.
REACTION Subject listening, thinking, reacting silently. Cuts over interview audio. Easy to forget — always capture before leaving.
ARCHIVAL Photos, documents, old footage. Must be cleared for rights. Scan or photograph at the highest resolution possible.
ATMOSPHERE Weather, crowds, environment, light. Sets emotional tone between sequences. Doesn't need to be directly related to content.
Essay Film — Key Concepts
VOICE
The essay film foregrounds a subjective voice — spoken narration (first person) or a strongly implied authorial perspective. The filmmaker is not invisible.
ASSOCIATION
Images connect through association, not causation. You don't have to show what the narrator describes — you can show something that rhymes with it, questions it, or contradicts it.
QUESTION
Essay films ask questions rather than answer them. A well-made essay film leaves the viewer with a richer problem, not a solution. Resist the urge to conclude too clearly.

Consent & ethics: Always be transparent about the project. Get written consent for anyone whose face or voice appears. Be especially careful with vulnerable subjects and anyone filmed in a personal context. When in doubt, check with your supervisor before filming.

Shoot Day Prep
The night before and the morning of — what to check so the day doesn't fall apart

The rule: Anything that can go wrong on set was preventable in prep. The shoot day itself is too late to discover a dead battery, a missing lens, or a location that's unavailable. Every item below is something a real production has lost a day to.

The Night Before
BATTERIES
Charge everything — camera batteries (charge all of them, not just one), recorder batteries, wireless transmitters, monitor, follow focus if motorised. Put them on charge before you sleep.
↳ Label batteries with tape so you know which are charged. Dead batteries look identical to charged ones.
MEDIA
Format cards and drives the night before — not on set. Confirm you have enough capacity. Calculate: shooting ratio (how many hours of material for each hour of finished film) × your planned shoot time × your codec's data rate.
↳ XF-AVC on the C300 at 4K/25fps ≈ 1 GB/min. A 128GB card = approx 2 hours. Bring more than you think you need.
EQUIPMENT
Pack your gear list the night before. Check each item off physically as you pack it. Lenses, cables (XLR, HDMI, power), adapters, memory cards, headphones, boom pole, clapper board, gaffer tape, lens wipes.
↳ Gaffer tape and cable ties solve a surprising percentage of on-set problems. Never leave without them.
DOCUMENTS
Print or download to your phone: call sheet, shot list, script with scene breakdowns, location address, cast and crew contact numbers. Phones die. Paper doesn't.
↳ Share the call sheet with every crew member and every cast member the night before — not the morning of.
LOCATION
Confirm location access with the owner or contact person. If you need a key, know who has it. Know where to park or where to drop equipment. Know the nearest power outlets.
↳ Walk the location in your head from the call sheet: how do you get in, where does camera go, where does sound set up, where does cast wait?
Morning of Shoot — Before Crew Arrives
1ST AD
The 1st AD should arrive before the crew to assess the space. Where does everything go? Are there any surprises — locked doors, noise sources, changed furniture? Brief the director before anyone else arrives.
CAMERA
Build the camera and confirm all settings before the first setup: frame rate (25fps), shutter (1/50), white balance (manual), recording format, audio routing. Do this while crew is setting up lighting — not during a take.
↳ Record a 30-second test clip and play it back. Confirm picture and audio are both recording correctly.
SOUND
Sound recordist sets up and does a full level check with the boom and each lavalier before the first setup. Confirm phantom power is on for condenser mics. Confirm timecode sync if using a separate recorder.
↳ Do a clap test: record a clap with picture and make sure the transient is visible in the audio waveform in playback.
BLOCKING
Before lighting, the director walks through the blocking with the actors (or stand-ins) for each setup. DP watches and notes where the camera needs to be. Lighting is planned around the blocking — not the other way around.
↳ Never finalise your lighting before you know exactly where the actors will move.
Sound File Sync — Camera + Recorder

When recording sound to a separate device (Zoom H5/H6, Sound Devices MixPre), you need to sync sound to picture in the edit. The clapper board is your sync point — the visual frame where the clapper closes matches the audio spike of the clap.

LABELLING
Sound files and camera files need a shared naming system. Convention: Scene_Shot_Take on the clapper board matches the folder or file name on the recorder. Example: board reads "S3 SH2 TK4" — recorder file is labelled S3-SH2-TK4.
↳ The script supervisor writes the board information and logs every take. Keep that log — it is the link between picture and sound.
SYNC POINT
In your NLE (DaVinci Resolve, Premiere): place the camera clip and sound clip on the timeline. Find the frame where the clapper closes — match it to the spike in the audio waveform. Most NLEs have auto-sync by waveform or timecode.
↳ DaVinci Resolve: right-click clips in the Media Pool → Auto-Sync Audio → Based on Waveform. Works without timecode.
MOS SHOTS
MOS = shots recorded without sound (camera movements, B-roll, inserts). Mark them on the board ("MOS") and in the sound log. Do not record to the external recorder for MOS — use only the camera's scratch audio.
↳ MOS stands for "mit out sound" — a phonetic German-English hybrid from early Hollywood. Now universally used.
BACKUP
At the end of each shoot day, copy all media to at least two separate locations before deleting anything from the cards. One drive can fail. Two drives failing simultaneously is extremely rare.
↳ The 3-2-1 rule: 3 copies, on 2 different media types, with 1 offsite. For student productions: camera card + primary drive + school server covers you.
Common Day-of Mishaps
Dead battery Always bring more than you think you need. Charge the night before. Rotate batteries — never put a depleted battery back in the bag without marking it.
Full card Format cards before every shoot. Keep one spare card sealed in the bag — only open it if needed. Label used cards so they don't get reformatted with footage on them.
Wrong frame rate Camera reset to 30fps or 24fps from previous use. Always check before the first shot of the day. A whole day at 30fps in a 25fps project is painful to fix.
No sound Forgot to press record on the recorder, or mic wasn't plugged in. Sound recordist calls "speed" when rolling — if you don't hear "speed" before "action", cut and check.
Late cast Build 30 minutes of buffer into your call times. Schedule lighting setups for the start of the day so camera and lighting can work while cast is in hair/makeup/travel.
Running out of time The 1st AD tracks time ruthlessly. Know your priority shots — if you fall behind, you drop the least important coverage, not the master shot. Always protect your wide.
Directing Tips
Practical tools for communicating with actors and running a set — not a theory course

These are pragmatic on-set tools, not a substitute for studying acting or directing methodology. The goal here is to help you avoid the most common communication breakdowns between a first-time director and their actors.

How to Give a Note
DO
Give physical actions and intentions — what the character is trying to do, achieve, or get from the other person. "You're trying to convince her to stay without letting her see you're scared." This gives the actor something playable.
↳ "What do you want from the other person in this scene?" is one of the most useful questions you can ask an actor before a take.
AVOID
Result direction — telling an actor how to feel ("be sadder", "be angrier", "be more nervous"). Emotions are a result of actions, not a starting point. When you direct results, actors push and perform the emotion rather than living it.
↳ "Be sadder" is not playable. "You're realising this is the last time you'll see her" is playable.
AVOID
Line readings — saying the line in the way you want it said and asking the actor to copy it. This destroys spontaneity. Instead, describe the subtext: "She's saying it's fine, but what she means is 'I will never forgive you for this'."
ONE NOTE
Give one note at a time. If you give an actor three notes before a take, they'll be thinking about all three instead of acting. Prioritise what matters most and give the other notes after the next take if needed.
PRIVATE
Give notes privately when possible — step away from the crew. Correcting an actor in front of the full set is embarrassing and breaks trust. A good working relationship with your cast is a technical asset, not a soft concern.
↳ Call "holding" and briefly walk to the actor. Two minutes of private conversation is worth 30 minutes of bad takes.
The Rehearsal on Set
BLOCKING
Walk through the physical movement of the scene with actors before lighting or rolling. This is for the director and DP to see what they're lighting and framing — not to lock in a perfect performance. Keep it loose.
↳ Let actors find their natural movement first, then adjust for camera if needed. Forced blocking looks forced on screen.
TECH RUN
A camera rehearsal with stand-ins (or the actors themselves at lower intensity) to set focus marks, lighting, and framing. Camera and sound crew need this — don't ask actors to give full performance energy during a tech run.
↳ Protect your actors' energy. The performance you want is for the take, not the rehearsal.
FIRST TAKE
Let the first take surprise you — don't over-direct before you've seen what the actor gives you. Sometimes the first take is the best take. You can always adjust, but you can't get that first spontaneous impulse back.
Common Actor Difficulties — Practical Responses
Actor is indicating / performing
They're showing you the emotion rather than living it — you can see the performance. Ask: "What does your character want right now in this moment?" Redirect to a specific, concrete objective rather than an emotional state.
Actor is inconsistent between takes
Natural in early takes — don't panic. Note the specific moment that worked best and ask them to repeat it. For blocking consistency: mark key positions on the floor with small tape marks so they hit focus marks reliably.
Actor is nervous or self-conscious
Reduce the number of people watching. Send non-essential crew out of eyeline. Give them something physical to do — an action, an object to handle. Nervous energy diminishes when attention is on a task rather than on performing.
Actor loses lines under pressure
Keep the script close. Don't make a big deal of it — line drops happen. You can cut and restart, or let them improvise through the moment and see if it works. Never show frustration — it makes it worse.
Non-professional / first-time actor
Use simpler, more concrete direction. "Walk to the table and pick up the cup" before "walk to the table." Reduce acting vocabulary — just describe what happens physically. Cast people close to the character — you can't direct someone to be something they're entirely not.

The director's job on set: You're not just making decisions about the image — you're managing the emotional safety of everyone in the room. A director who is calm, clear, and respectful gets better performances than one who is anxious or impatient, regardless of talent. The set takes its temperature from the director.
