Shot Sizes
Standard framing — use these terms on your shot list and on set
EWS Extreme Wide Shot Subject tiny in landscape. Establishes place, scale, isolation.
WS Wide / Full Shot Full body with space around. Shows subject in environment.
MLS Medium Long Shot Knees to top of head. Character and some environment.
MS Medium Shot Waist to top of head. Most common dialogue shot.
MCU Medium Close-Up Chest to top of head. Conversational intimacy.
CU Close-Up Face fills frame. Emotion and reaction.
ECU Extreme Close-Up Single feature: eyes, hands, mouth. Intensity and detail.
OTS Over-the-Shoulder Camera behind one character looking at another.
POV Point of View Camera = character's exact eyeline. Subjective.
INS Insert Shot Close-up of object carrying story information.
2S Two-Shot Two subjects in frame. Relationship and dynamic.
EST Establishing Shot Opens a scene. Shows where and when we are.

The 180° Rule: Draw an imaginary line through your two subjects. Keep the camera on one side of this line for the entire scene. Crossing it disorients the viewer spatially — this is a continuity error, not a style choice.

Camera Movement
Note movement type on every shot in your shot list
STATIC
Camera locked off on tripod. No movement at all. The dominant shot type in professional cinema — stillness is power, not laziness.
↳ Default choice. Always ask: does this shot need to move?
PAN
Camera head rotates horizontally left or right on a fixed tripod. Follows action, reveals space, connects subjects. Camera does not translate — it pivots.
↳ Pan speed matters enormously. Too fast = whip pan. Too slow = uncomfortable.
TILT
Camera head rotates vertically up or down on a fixed tripod. Reveals scale (tilt up a building), follows action vertically, creates dread or awe.
↳ Often combined with other movements — tilt + pan simultaneously.
DOLLY
Camera physically moves through space on a wheeled cart along tracks. Moving the entire camera toward (push in) or away (pull out) from subject. Creates parallax — background shifts relative to foreground.
↳ Zoom changes focal length. Dolly changes perspective. Not the same thing.
TRACK
Camera moves laterally alongside a moving subject — parallel tracking. Keeps subject in frame while background flows past. Feeling of accompaniment, momentum.
↳ Tracking shots require laid track or a very smooth floor. Plan ahead.
HANDHELD
Operator holds camera without stabilisation. Introduces organic wobble, energy, urgency, or unease. The camera becomes a participant in the scene, not an observer.
↳ Intentional handheld ≠ accidentally shaky. Decide before you pick it up.
STEADICAM
Camera mounted on a spring-loaded vest rig worn by the operator. Smooth, gliding movement that follows action through complex spaces. Neither locked-off static nor unstable handheld — its own distinct vocabulary.
↳ Requires specialist operator. Don't improvise this on shoot day.
CRANE / JIB
Camera elevated on a counterbalanced arm. Reveals environment from above, suggests scale, provides God's-eye view. Often used at the end of a scene to pull away from characters.
↳ Even a simple jib arm creates dramatic elevation changes in small spaces.
WHIP PAN
An extremely fast pan that blurs the frame during movement. Used as a transition between scenes or to suggest rapid disorientation. Edgar Wright uses it extensively for comedic and rhythmic effect.
↳ Match direction of blur when cutting to a reverse whip pan in editing.
DUTCH ANGLE
Camera tilted on its longitudinal axis — the horizon is diagonal in frame. Suggests psychological unease, instability, moral ambiguity. A stylistic choice, not a correctable error.
↳ Use sparingly — it draws attention to itself. Earn it with context.
On-Set Roles
Who does what — and who answers to whom

Chain of command: On a TVP102 set, every crew member takes direction from their department head. Don't give notes across departments — that's the Director and 1st AD's job. If something is wrong, tell your department head first.

DIRECTION
Director Overall creative authority. Communicates vision to all departments. Gives direction to actors. The only person who says "Cut" (or delegates this to 1st AD).
1st AD Runs the floor day-to-day. Calls "Action", "Cut", "Rolling". Manages schedule, safety, and communication between departments. If the Director is the creative brain, the 1st AD is the operational spine.
PRODUCTION
Producer Manages logistics, budget, and schedule off-camera. Problem-solves before problems reach the set. Produces the call sheet with the 1st AD.
Script Supervisor Monitors continuity shot-to-shot. Logs completed takes, circle takes, and timing. The editor's best friend — their notes are what the edit room works from.
CAMERA
DP / DoP Director of Photography. Responsible for all visual and lighting decisions. Translates the director's vision into specific shots, lighting setups, and lens choices.
Camera Operator Physically operates the camera during takes under the DP's guidance. On small productions, the DP often also operates.
1st AC (Focus Puller) Manages focus during shots, measures actor distances, and maintains the camera. One of the most technically demanding on-set roles.
DIT / Data Wrangler Manages all media cards and file backup during shooting. Never leave a shoot day without two copies of the footage on separate drives.
LIGHTING & GRIP
Gaffer Chief electrician. Executes the DP's lighting plan. Responsible for all electrical safety on set.
Key Grip Manages all non-electrical physical support: dolly tracks, rigging, camera mounts, reflectors, flags. Works closely with the Gaffer.
SOUND
Sound Recordist / Mixer Captures all on-set audio. Monitors dialogue and ambient levels. Sets recording levels and manages the audio recording device.
Boom Operator Holds and positions the boom microphone to capture dialogue as close as possible without entering frame. A skilled boom op is invisible but invaluable.
ART DEPARTMENT
Production Designer Responsible for the entire visual world of the film: sets, props, dressing. Works from the director's vision and the DP's lighting plan.
Costume Designer All wardrobe decisions. Maintains costume continuity between scenes — especially critical when scenes are shot out of order.
Set Commands
The language of the floor — know these before day one
Shooting Sequence
QUIET ON SET Called by 1st AD before a take. All talking, phones, and movement stops immediately.
TURNOVER / ROLL 1st AD tells camera and sound to begin recording. Both must confirm before action is called.
ROLLING Camera operator confirms the camera is recording. "Rolling" = camera is running.
SPEED Sound Recordist confirms audio is recording. Comes after "Rolling", before "Action".
MARK IT Clapperboard is hit in front of camera to create a sync point for picture and sound in post.
ACTION Director (or 1st AD at director's instruction) calls this. Actors begin the scene. Not before.
CUT Director ends the take. No one moves until this is called. Camera and sound keep running until "Cut".
CHECK THE GATE After the last take of a setup, the 1st AC inspects the camera for dust or debris. Nothing moves until this is cleared.
MOVING ON 1st AD confirms the gate is good and the shot is complete. Crew begins striking and setting up the next shot.
BACK TO ONE Reset. Actors return to their starting positions. Crew resets props and continuity to the top of the scene.
Useful Terms
STICKS UP Camera is on the tripod. As opposed to handheld or on a rig.
HOT POINTS Warning called when carrying something with extended ends (lighting stand, dolly track) through a crowd.
TALENT On-set shorthand for the actors / performers.
CRAFTY Craft services — the food and drinks table. A very serious priority on any set.
MARTINI The last shot of the day. Named for what comes next.
ABBY SINGER The second-to-last shot of the day. Named after a real production manager.
ROOM TONE Record 30–60 seconds of silence at the end of every location. The sound of the room at rest. Essential for dialogue editing.
WILD TRACK Sound recorded without picture rolling — dialogue, effects, or ambience captured for use in post.
Exposure
The three variables that control how light hits your sensor
ISO Sensor Sensitivity Higher ISO = more light sensitivity = more digital noise/grain. Always shoot at your camera's base ISO when possible. Base ISO: 800 typical
Safe range: 100–3200
f/ Aperture Low f-stop (f/1.8) = wide open = shallow depth of field + more light. High f-stop (f/11) = narrow = deep focus + less light. Shallow DOF: f/1.4–f/2.8
Deep focus: f/8–f/16
1/ Shutter Speed Controls duration of exposure and motion blur. The 180° rule: shutter speed = 2× frame rate for natural-looking motion. 24fps → 1/48s (use 1/50)
25fps → 1/50s
The 180° Shutter Rule
Shutter Speed = Frame Rate × 2
Shooting at 25fps? Set shutter to 1/50. Shooting at 24fps? Set to 1/48 (or 1/50). This gives natural motion blur — the look of cinema.

White balance tip: Set manually, never auto — auto WB drifts between takes and creates continuity problems in the colour grade. Shoot a grey card or white card at the start of each lighting setup and use it to correct in post.

Depth of Field Factors
APERTURE Wider aperture (lower f-stop) = shallower DOF. Most direct control over background blur.
FOCAL LENGTH Longer lens (85mm, 135mm) = shallower DOF. Wide lens (24mm) = deeper DOF at same f-stop.
DISTANCE Closer subject to camera = shallower DOF. Move in before reaching for a wider aperture.
SENSOR SIZE Full-frame sensors produce shallower DOF than crop sensors at equivalent settings.
Lighting
Types, positions, and practical setups
Three-Point Lighting

The foundation of narrative lighting. Not a formula to follow blindly — a starting point to understand and then break deliberately. Key → Fill → Back/Rim. Master this and you can light anything.

KEY LIGHT
Primary light source. Determines the direction and mood of the scene. Usually 45° to one side and slightly above the subject. The dominant light.
FILL LIGHT
Softens the shadow created by the key. Placed on the opposite side, lower intensity. Controls contrast ratio — how dark the shadow side is.
BACK / RIM LIGHT
Behind and above the subject, aimed at their shoulders and hair. Separates them from the background. Creates depth. Often the most cinematic of the three.
PRACTICAL
A light source visible in frame — a lamp, a window, a screen. Also used to mean any on-set light motivated by a visible source in the scene world.
BOUNCE
Light reflected off a white card, foam board, or reflector. Softer, more diffuse than direct light. Free and available — a bounce board changes everything.
NEGATIVE FILL
A black card placed to subtract light from the shadow side of a face, increasing contrast. Darkens without adding a light source.
Light Quality
HARD LIGHT
Small, direct source creates sharp-edged shadows. Dramatic, high-contrast. Direct sun, bare bulb, undiffused LED.
↳ Use for tension, intensity, noir. Unflattering on skin at close range.
SOFT LIGHT
Large or diffused source wraps around subject, creates gradual shadows. Flattering, gentle. Overcast sky, light through diffusion, bounced light.
↳ Larger the source relative to subject = softer the light.
Contrast Ratio

The ratio between key and fill light. 1:1 = flat, even (news broadcast). 2:1 = gentle modelling. 4:1 = dramatic narrative look. 8:1+ = noir / high contrast. Know your ratio before you light.

Continuity Checklist
Check these between every take — tap to mark as done, reset between setups
Costume & Props
Clothing position — collar, buttons, jacket lapels, sleeves. Are they exactly as they were at the start of the scene?
Hair — loose strands, parting, position of any accessories. Check against last shot's reference photo.
Props in hand — which hand, how held, level of any drink in a glass, state of any food item.
Props on set — position of every object in frame. Take a reference photo before every setup change.
Jewellery & accessories — rings, watches, glasses, earrings. These disappear between takes more often than anything else.
Performance
Action matches — if cutting mid-action (picking up a cup, turning to look), the action must match from both angles precisely.
Eyeline — actors must look at the same point in space across angles. Mark an eyeline target if needed.
180° rule respected — all camera positions on the same side of the action line throughout the scene.
Technical
Exposure locked — if shooting near a window, block light changes or use manual exposure to prevent flicker between takes.
White balance set manually — auto WB drifts. Set a fixed value at the start of each lighting setup.
Sound speed confirmed — "Speed" called before every "Action". Never start a take without audio rolling.
Clapper board updated — scene number, shot number, and take number correct before every take.
Media card capacity — check remaining recording time before a long take. Back up completed cards before they're reused.
End of Setup
Check the gate — 1st AC inspects camera before moving on. Never strike a setup before this is done.
Reference photo taken — script supervisor or continuity photographs actors and set before any changes.
Script supervisor notes updated — take numbers, circle takes, timing, and any continuity notes logged before moving on.
Formats & Specs
Aspect ratios, frame rates, and delivery specifications
Aspect Ratios
1.78:1 16:9 — Standard HD television and streaming. Most cameras' default.
1.85:1 Flat — Standard US theatrical widescreen. Very common for short films.
2.39:1 Scope / Anamorphic — Ultra-wide cinematic. Dramatic, epic, expansive.
4:3 Academy — Vintage feel. Used stylistically by Wes Anderson, others.
1:1 Square — Uncommon. Strong stylistic statement. Instagram-native.
9:16 Vertical — Mobile-native. Occasionally used for vertical film formats.
Frame Rates
24 fps The cinematic standard. The motion blur and cadence of 24fps is what audiences associate with "film". Used by default for narrative short films worldwide.
25 fps European broadcast standard (PAL). Very close to 24fps visually. Set shutter to 1/50. The correct default for TVP102 films intended for Norwegian broadcast or streaming.
30 fps US broadcast standard (NTSC). Looks slightly more "video" than 24/25fps. Used for news, sports, some web content.
48–120 fps High frame rates. Used for slow-motion playback. Record at 60fps or 120fps to play back at 25fps for 2.4× or 4.8× slow motion respectively.
Codec & Delivery Quick Reference
H.264 / H.265 Compressed delivery codecs. Small file sizes, widely compatible. Use for online submission, streaming delivery, and any format where file size matters.
ProRes 422 Apple's editing and delivery codec. Large files but excellent quality. Use for intermediate editing or when delivering to a broadcast platform.
RAW Unprocessed sensor data. Maximum post-production flexibility but very large files. Requires more processing power in the edit. Used when colour grade is a priority.
DCP Digital Cinema Package — the format used for theatrical projection. If submitting to a film festival with cinema screening, you may need to create a DCP from your master file.

TVP102 delivery note: Unless specified otherwise by your supervisor, deliver your exam film as an H.264 .mp4 at 1920×1080 (1080p), 25fps, with stereo audio at -3dB peak. Keep your original project files and master export until after grades are confirmed.
